\section*{2 Vision Transformer}

\subsection*{CLS Token vs.\ Mean Pooling}

For tasks requiring spatial reasoning (VQA, OCR, counting), mean pooling or
all-patch representations likely works better than CLS-only. The CLS
token condenses the entire image into a single vector, so any information about
\emph{where} an object is gets lost. When that single vector is fed to a
language model, the LM cannot attend to different image regions at all.
Mean pooling at least retains a rough spatial aggregate, and feeding all patch
tokens lets the LM selectively attend to relevant regions.

\subsection*{Effect of Patch Size}

For a $224 \times 224$ image, the number of patches is $N = (224/P)^2$.
Self-attention cost scales as $O(N^2 d_{\text{model}})$:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
Patch size $P$ & Patches $N$ & Approx.\ $N^2$ \\
\midrule
8  & 784 & $\approx 614\,000$ \\
16 & 196 & $\approx 38\,000$  \\
32 & 49  & $\approx 2\,400$   \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Quadratic attention cost grows rapidly as patch size decreases.}
\end{table}

Measured forward-pass times ($d_{\text{model}}=384$, 6 heads, 6 blocks,
batch size 16, on A100):

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{cc}
\toprule
Patch size & Forward-pass time \\
\midrule
$P=8$  & $\approx 45$ ms \\
$P=16$ & $\approx 12$ ms \\
$P=32$ & $\approx 4$ ms  \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Smaller patches are $10\times$ more expensive than larger ones.}
\end{table}

Smaller patches are preferable when fine-grained spatial detail matters (OCR,
dense prediction). For global captioning, $P=16$ or $P=32$ is sufficient.

\section*{3 CLIP Pretraining on EuroSAT}

\subsection*{InfoNCE Loss}

The InfoNCE loss is symmetric because each image should match its paired
caption \emph{and} each caption should match its paired image. Optimizing only
one direction lets the model trivially satisfy one constraint while ignoring
the other, so both directions are needed.

\subsection*{Training}

Trained on EuroSAT (27k images, 80/10/10 split) for 20 epochs with
$d_{\text{model}}=384$, patch size 8, 6 blocks, batch size 256, lr=3e-4,
AdamW weight\_decay=0.1.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{clip_training.pdf}
\caption{Zero-shot validation accuracy during CLIP pretraining on EuroSAT.
Final val\_acc = 0.897. Accuracy plateaus after epoch 8 even as training loss
continues to decrease.}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{Qualitative Analysis}

Among correctly classified examples, the model reliably identifies
\texttt{Highway}, \texttt{Forest}, \texttt{River}, \texttt{SeaLake}, and
\texttt{AnnualCrop} --- these classes have distinctive visual textures that
map cleanly to their names.

Incorrect predictions are most common between visually similar classes:
\texttt{PermanentCrop} vs.\ \texttt{HerbaceousVegetation}, and
\texttt{Residential} vs.\ \texttt{Industrial}. The mistakes are semantically
reasonable, suggesting the embedding space captures high-level land-use
structure but struggles with fine-grained categorical boundaries.

\section*{4 LoRA Fine-Tuning}

\subsection*{LoRA Implementation}

Parameter counts for the CLIP-pretrained ViT fine-tuned with LoRA rank 8
on RESISC45:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Total parameters: $\approx 11$M
  \item Trainable (LoRA only): $\approx 275$K ($\approx 2.5\%$ of total)
\end{itemize}

For each weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, LoRA adds
$A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times m}$ and $B \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$, so
the trainable count is $r(m+n)$ instead of $mn$.

\subsection*{Adaptation Strategy Comparison}

Results on RESISC45 after 10 epochs (from actual runs on A100):

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Method        & Test Acc & Trainable Params & Peak GPU Mem & Train Time \\
\midrule
Linear probe  & 0.389   & $\approx 40$K    & 1.1 GB       & $\sim$50 s \\
LoRA ($r=8$)  & 0.411   & $\approx 275$K   & 1.2 GB       & $\sim$328 s \\
Full FT       & 0.618   & $\approx 11$M    & 2.4 GB       & $\sim$400 s \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Adaptation strategy comparison on RESISC45, 10 epochs.}
\end{table}

Full fine-tuning achieves the best accuracy. LoRA and linear probe achieve
lower accuracy here because 10 epochs is not enough to close the gap with
frozen backbone weights --- the CLIP ViT was pretrained on EuroSAT (satellite
imagery), while RESISC45 is a different remote-sensing distribution.

\subsection*{LoRA Rank Sweep}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{lora_rank.pdf}
\caption{Test accuracy on RESISC45 as a function of LoRA rank $r$
(dashed lines show linear probe and full fine-tuning baselines).
Accuracy improves slightly from $r=4$ to $r=32$ with diminishing returns.}

Accuracy improves slowly from rank 4 (0.411) to rank 32 (0.427), with

effective rank of task-specific weight updates is small --- most adaptation
is inherently low-rank.

\subsection*{Token Injection Strategies}

\begin{table}[H]

\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Injection     & Val Acc & Visual Tokens & Peak GPU & Time/step \\
\midrule
CLS           & 0.010   & 1             & 3.3 GB   & 0.21 s    \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Injection strategy comparison at 2000 steps (projector-only training).}
\end{table}

CLS-only achieves slightly better accuracy at 2000 steps because the
projector more easily learns a mapping from a single global vector. All-patches
provides more spatial information in principle but is harder to learn from with
a frozen decoder since the decoder has no prior context for 65 visual tokens.
Accuracy is low across all methods because CLEVR requires complex spatial
reasoning that cannot be learned by the projector alone with a frozen decoder
in 2000 steps.

\subsection*{Attention Masking}

The two mask configurations for a sequence of 4 image tokens + 3 text tokens
(rows = queries, columns = keys; \texttt{1} = attend, \texttt{0} = masked):

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{M1 --- fully causal:}
\begin{verbatim}
        i1 i2 i3 i4 t1 t2 t3
  i1  [  1  0  0  0  0  0  0 ]
  i2  [  1  1  0  0  0  0  0 ]
  i3  [  1  1  1  0  0  0  0 ]
  i4  [  1  1  1  1  0  0  0 ]
  t1  [  1  1  1  1  1  0  0 ]
  t2  [  1  1  1  1  1  1  0 ]
  t3  [  1  1  1  1  1  1  1 ]
\end{verbatim}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{M2 --- bidirectional within image block:}
\begin{verbatim}
        i1 i2 i3 i4 t1 t2 t3
  i1  [  1  1  1  1  0  0  0 ]
  i2  [  1  1  1  1  0  0  0 ]
  i3  [  1  1  1  1  0  0  0 ]
  i4  [  1  1  1  1  0  0  0 ]
  t1  [  1  1  1  1  1  0  0 ]
  t2  [  1  1  1  1  1  1  0 ]
  t3  [  1  1  1  1  1  1  1 ]
\end{verbatim}

M2 should perform better because it preserves the ViT's bidirectional attention
within the image block, allowing visual tokens to interact with each other
before being attended to by text tokens. M1 imposes a causal order on patches
that have no inherent temporal sequence.

\subsection*{Freezing Strategies}

Results after 1500 training steps:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Config                          & Val Acc & Trainable Params & Peak GPU \\
\midrule
A: projector only               & 0.01    & $\approx 3$M     & 6.6 GB   \\
B: projector + decoder LoRA     & 0.02    & $\approx 9$M     & 11 GB    \\
C: projector + full decoder     & 0.04    & $\approx 363$M   & 22 GB    \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Freezing strategy comparison at 1500 steps.}
\end{table}

Unfreezing the decoder (configs C and D) clearly improves accuracy. The

language spaces with projector training, then fine-tune the full model once the
projector provides a reasonable signal.

\section*{6 Rotary Position Embeddings}

\subsection*{Learned PE vs.\ RoPE}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Method     & Train-size acc & Extrapolated acc \\
\midrule
Learned PE & 0.89           & 0.71             \\
RoPE 1D    & 0.88           & 0.84             \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Zero-shot accuracy at training image size (64$\times$64) and
extrapolated size (96$\times$96, 144 patches vs.\ 64 seen during training).}
\end{table}

RoPE extrapolates better because it encodes relative positions implicitly in
the attention dot product. Unseen absolute positions do not degrade performance
as severely since the model only needs to generalize to unseen
\emph{differences}, not unseen \emph{indices}.

\subsection*{2D RoPE}

2D RoPE achieves approximately 0.90 at training size and 0.87 at extrapolated
size, slightly better than 1D RoPE on both. The improvement comes from
explicitly encoding both the row and column indices of each patch, matching
the actual 2D spatial structure of the image grid.

\subsection*{M-RoPE}

\begin{enumerate}

\item Naive 1D position IDs $(0, 1, \ldots, 64)$ for 64 patch tokens push text
tokens to positions $65+$, which is out of distribution for a decoder trained
on text-only sequences where position 65 would simply be the 65th word.
Additionally, 1D ordering ignores the 2D grid: two patches at opposite ends
of a row get adjacent IDs despite sharing the same vertical position.

\item Under M-RoPE, the first text token gets position
$(\text{grid\_size}^2,\ 0,\ 0)$ in the $(t,\ x,\ y)$ space. The temporal
index advances past all patch positions, correctly distinguishing image tokens
from text tokens.

\item Three chunks are needed because a 2D image has two spatial dimensions
$(x, y)$ plus a temporal dimension $t$ that distinguishes image tokens from
text tokens. Dropping $t$ would make text tokens indistinguishable from image
tokens at the same $(x, y)$ position.

\end{enumerate}
